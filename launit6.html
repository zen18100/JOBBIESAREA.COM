Data Visualization and Interpretation

Information from Visual Sources

Mastering the technical skills required to use, present, and analyze data contained within tables, figures, and diagrams.

1. Use and Value of Visual Sources in Communication

Visual sources (tables, graphs, diagrams, and figures) are essential tools in technical communication. They help the audience quickly process complex data and relationships that would be cumbersome to explain in text alone.

1.1 Why Use Visual Aids?

  • Clarity and Impact: Complex data trends (e.g., exponential growth) are instantly recognizable in a line graph, unlike in a raw list of numbers.
  • Space Saving: A table can summarize hundreds of data points in less space than paragraphs describing those same points.
  • Increased Retention: Readers tend to remember information presented visually longer than information presented purely verbally.
  • Highlighting Relationships: Figures like schematics or flowcharts clearly show connections and structure between components or steps.

1.2 When to Choose a Visual Aid

  • To show trends or changes over time (Line Graphs).
  • To show distribution or parts of a whole (Pie Charts).
  • To show comparisons between discrete categories (Bar Graphs).
  • To organize detailed, precise, and numerical data (Tables).
  • To show physical structure, connectivity, or process steps (Diagrams and Flowcharts).

2. Description of Tables and Figures

A visual source should never "stand alone." It must be introduced, referenced, and explained within the body text to guide the reader's attention.

2.1 Referencing Rules

  1. Introduce Before Use: Always mention the table or figure in the text before it appears.
  2. Specific Reference: Use the full title or number when referencing (e.g., "As demonstrated in Figure 2,..." or "The results are summarized in Table 1.1...").
  3. Do Not Repeat Data: Do not simply list all the data points from the visual. Instead, refer the reader to the visual and discuss its main implications (see Section 4).

2.2 Captioning and Labeling Conventions

  • Tables: Are numbered consecutively and labeled above the table (e.g., Table 1: Quarterly Sales by Region).
  • Figures: Are numbered consecutively and labeled below the figure (e.g., Figure 3: Exploded View of Turbine Assembly). Figures include all graphs, diagrams, charts, photographs, and drawings.
  • Axis/Labels: Every axis on a graph must be clearly labeled with the variable and its unit of measurement.
Correct In-Text Reference

The efficiency dropped significantly in the third hour, as indicated by the steep decline shown in Figure 2.

3. Presentation of Information in Tables and Figures

Good presentation minimizes effort for the reader and maximizes understanding.

3.1 Table Presentation

  • Clear Headings: Column and row headings must be concise and informative.
  • Units: Units of measurement should be placed in the column headings, not repeated in every cell.
  • Alignment: Align numbers by the decimal point or right-justify them. Align text left.
  • Consistency: Maintain consistent precision (e.g., always use two decimal places).
Example: Well-Formed Technical Table
Table 1: Monthly Power Consumption (kWh)
Month Baseline (kWh) Current Use (kWh) Difference (%)
Jan 1,500.5 1,450.0 -3.4%
Feb 1,480.0 1,550.8 +4.8%
Mar 1,510.3 1,505.0 -0.4%
Note: The baseline is derived from the previous year's average monthly usage.

3.2 Figure Presentation (Graphs and Charts)

A properly constructed graph simplifies complex relationships. For example, a scatter plot is excellent for showing correlation.

  • Visual Simplicity: Avoid excessive colors, complex background patterns, or unnecessary 3D effects that distract from the data.
  • Axis Scales: Start the scale at zero unless a break is necessary (and clearly indicated) to avoid misleading the reader.
  • Legend: If the visual uses multiple colors or line types (series), a clear legend must be provided.
  • Data Labels: Use data labels sparingly; they are usually only needed if the exact value is critical and difficult to read from the axis.

4. Interpretation of Tables and Figures

Interpretation involves moving beyond merely stating the data and analyzing what the data means in the context of the document's purpose.

4.1 Analytical Steps

  1. State the Focus: Tell the reader what the visual is about (e.g., "Table 1 compares the material stress points of Alloys A and B.").
  2. Identify the Main Trend/Pattern: What is the most important takeaway? (e.g., "The primary observation is the inverse relationship between temperature and viscosity.")
  3. Highlight the Extremes: Point out the highest and lowest values (e.g., "Alloy A showed the maximum tensile strength at 220 MPa, while Alloy B performed the lowest at 180 MPa.").
  4. Note Anomalies: Mention any unexpected or unusual data points that deviate from the established trend.
  5. Draw Conclusions (Relate to Text): Explain the significance of the data and how it supports or contradicts your main argument in the surrounding text.
Example: Interpreting Table 1 (Monthly Power Consumption)
  1. State the Focus: The analysis centers on the variance between the facility's current power consumption and its historical baseline, measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh).
  2. Identify the Main Trend/Pattern: The overall consumption remained relatively stable, hovering around the baseline of 1,500 kWh, indicating consistent energy usage across the quarter.
  3. Highlight the Extremes: The maximum deviation occurred in February, showing a +4.8% increase over the baseline. January recorded the largest reduction at -3.4%.
  4. Note Anomalies: The significant increase in February requires further investigation into equipment logs or weather data to determine the specific cause.
  5. Draw Conclusions (Relate to Text): While two out of three months met or exceeded efficiency goals, the February spike indicates that the current energy management protocol is inconsistent under specific environmental conditions (likely cold weather operations) and requires a targeted review.
The interpretation section is where you connect the isolated data points back to your larger technical argument or report conclusion.
