**Chapter 3: Kinematics**

Kinematics is the branch of mechanics that describes the **motion of objects** in relation to time, **without** considering the forces or causes (like pushes or pulls) of that motion. We treat moving objects as a **particle** (a point-like object) to simplify the analysis.

1. **Fundamentals of Motion**

1.1 Motion Definition

An object is in **motion** if its position continuously changes with respect to a chosen reference frame (its surroundings). The path it follows is called its **trajectory**.

1.2 Types of Motion

  • **One-Dimensional (1D) Motion:** Motion along a **straight line**, requiring only one coordinate (like x or y) to describe its position.
    Example: A car moving on a straight road.
  • **Two-Dimensional (2D) Motion:** Motion in a **plane**, requiring two coordinates (x, y) to track the object.
    Example: A frisbee flying or a boat on a lake.

2. **Motion in One Dimension (1D)**

2.1 Distance (Scalar) and Displacement (x⇀, Vector)

The Distance is the **actual length of the path** traversed (a **Scalar**). The Displacement is the **change in position** (a **Vector**).

Displacement x⇀ = x_f - x_i

Clarification: Distance vs. Displacement

If a person walks 10 m East and 10 m West, they return to their starting point.

  • **Total Distance:** 10 m + 10 m = **20 m**.
  • **Total Displacement (x⇀):** 10 m - 10 m = **0 m**.

2.2 Speed (Scalar) and Velocity (v⇀, Vector)

(a) Average Speed

The rate of distance covered over time.

Average speed = Total distance / Time interval

(b) Average Velocity (v⇀)

The rate of **displacement** over time (a **Vector**).

Average velocity = Displacement / Time interval = Δx / Δt

(c) Instantaneous Velocity (v⇀)

The velocity at an **exact moment**. This is the derivative of position (x) with respect to time (t):

v⇀ = dx / dt

2.3 Acceleration (a⇀, Vector)

Acceleration is the time rate of **change of velocity**. It is a **Vector** with units of m/s².

Average acceleration = Change in velocity / Time interval = Δv / Δt

When the direction of acceleration is opposite to the direction of velocity, the object is slowing down, which is called **deceleration**.

3. **Uniformly Accelerated Motion (UAM)**

This is motion where the **acceleration (a)** is **constant** (or uniform) over the entire time interval. This simplifies the analysis greatly.

3.1 The Kinematic Equations (When acceleration 'a' is constant)

These four equations relate initial velocity (v_0), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), time (t), and displacement (s).

(i) Velocity-Time Relation

v = v_0 + a t

(ii) Position-Time Relation (Displacement)

s = v_0 t + 0.5 a t²

(iii) Position-Velocity Relation (Time-Independent)

v² = v_0² + 2 a s

(iv) Displacement based on Average Velocity

s = ( (v + v_0) / 2 ) t

Example: Stopping a Car

A car is traveling at 30 m/s (v_0) and brakes with a uniform deceleration of -6.0 m/s² (a). What distance (s) does the car travel before coming to a complete stop (v = 0 m/s)?

  • **Given:** v_0 = 30 m/s, v = 0 m/s, a = -6.0 m/s².
  • **Equation Choice:** Use v² = v_0² + 2 a s.
    • 0² = 30² + 2 x (-6.0) x s
    0 = 900 - 12 s
    12 s = 900
    s = 900 / 12 = **75 m**

**Result:** The car travels **75 m** before stopping.

3.2 Special Case: Free Fall Motion

This occurs when the object's acceleration is due to gravity (g), approximately 9.8 m/s². Using the upward direction as positive means the acceleration is $a = -g$.

Free Fall Equations (using $a = -g$)

v = v_0 - g t
y = y_0 + v_0 t - 0.5 g t²
v² = v_0² - 2 g ( y - y_0 )

4. **Motion in Two Dimensions (2D)**

2D motion is broken down into two independent 1D motions: one along the **x-direction** and one along the **y-direction**. The quantity that links these two is **time** ($t$).

4.1 Vector Components

All vector quantities in 2D are expressed using unit vectors i⇀ (x-axis) and j⇀ (y-axis).

  • **Position Vector (r⇀):** r⇀ = x i⇀ + y j⇀
  • **Velocity Vector (v⇀):** v⇀ = v_x i⇀ + v_y j⇀
  • **Acceleration Vector (a⇀):** a⇀ = a_x i⇀ + a_y j⇀

The magnitude (size) of the velocity vector is found using the Pythagorean theorem:

Magnitude v = sqrt ( v_x² + v_y² )

5. **Projectile Motion (A 2D Example)**

A **projectile** is any object moving under the influence of gravity alone. Its path is always a **parabola**.

5.1 Key Principles

  • **Horizontal Motion (x):** Acceleration is **zero** ($a_x = 0$). The velocity $v_x$ is **constant**.
  • **Vertical Motion (y):** Acceleration is constant: $a_y = -g$ (The motion is uniformly accelerated).

5.2 Initial Velocity Components

If a projectile is launched with initial speed $v_0$ at an angle $\emptyset$:

v_0x = v_0 cos(∅)
v_0y = v_0 sin(∅)

5.3 Important Projectile Parameters

Assuming the object lands at the same height it was launched from ($y_0=y=0$).

  • **Time of Flight (T):** Total time in the air.
    T = ( 2 v_0 sin(∅) ) / g
  • **Maximum Height (H):** The highest point reached.
    H = ( v_0² sin²(∅) ) / ( 2 g )
  • **Horizontal Range (R):** The total horizontal distance traveled.
    R = ( v_0² sin(2 ∅) ) / g

    The maximum range is achieved when the launch angle ∅ is **45°**.

Example: Projectile Range

A football is kicked with an initial speed of 20 m/s (v_0) at an angle of 30° ($\emptyset$). Find its horizontal range (R), using g = 10 m/s² and sin(2 x 30°) = sin(60°) $\approx$ 0.866.

  • **Given:** v_0 = 20 m/s, $\emptyset$ = 30°, g = 10 m/s².
  • **Equation:** Use the Range formula.
    • R = ( v_0² sin(2 ∅) ) / g
    R = ( 20² sin(2 x 30°) ) / 10
    R = ( 400 x sin(60°) ) / 10
    R = ( 400 x 0.866 ) / 10
    R = 346.4 / 10 = **34.64 m**

**Result:** The horizontal range is approximately **34.64 m**.
