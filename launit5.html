UNIT 4: Technical Writing (Definitions)

1. Definitions (The Core Purpose)

In technical communication, a definition is more than just a dictionary entry; it is a precise statement that specifies the meaning, limits, and function of a term for a specific audience.

1.1 Why Define Terms?

Technical documents must be unambiguous. Definitions ensure:

  • Clarity: Eliminates confusion about specialized concepts.
  • Accuracy: Establishes a common, precise meaning for experts and laypersons alike.
  • Consistency: Ensures the term is used in the same way throughout the document and organization.
  • Reader Accessibility: Allows the non-expert reader to follow complex instructions or arguments.

2. When and What to Define

The golden rule of technical writing: Define terms based on your audience and the context of your document.

2.1 When to Define

  • First Appearance: Always define a technical term the first time it is used in the document.
  • Ambiguity: If a term has multiple meanings (even outside the technical field), define the specific meaning you are using.
  • New/Unfamiliar Terms: When introducing novel concepts, abbreviations (acronyms), or jargon that the target audience may not know.
  • Specialized Usage: If you are using a common word (e.g., "power," "load," "system") in a highly specific, technical way.

2.2 What to Define

  • Jargon: Highly specialized language specific to your field (e.g., Polymorphism in programming).
  • Acronyms/Abbreviations: Always provide the full term before the abbreviation in parentheses (e.g., Central Processing Unit (CPU)).
  • Measurements: When using non-standard or unfamiliar units of measure.
  • Proprietary Terms: Product names or internal system names unique to your organization.

3. How to Define (The Logical Pattern)

The classical method of definition is the most common and effective way to ensure clarity. It follows a simple, three-part structure.

[Image of the Three-Part Structure of Definition]

3.1 The Three-Part Structure

A strong definition consists of the Term, the Class, and the Differentiating Characteristics:

  1. Term: The word or phrase being defined.
  2. Class (or Group): The broad category or group the term belongs to.
  3. Distinguishing Characteristics: The specific qualities, limits, or features that make this term unique within its class.
Example Definition Construction (Term: Inductance)

Term: Inductance
Class: is a property of an electrical conductor
Distinguishing Characteristics: that opposes a change in the current flow through it.

Final Sentence: Inductance is a property of an electrical conductor that opposes a change in the current flow through it.

4. Forms of Definitions

Definitions can be presented in short, inline formats, or expanded into long, detailed explanations, depending on the audience's needs.

4.1 Parenthetical Definition (Briefest Form)

This is the shortest form, placing a synonym or brief phrase within parentheses immediately after the term's first use. Best for simple terms or non-essential jargon.

The engineer used a potentiometer (variable resistor) to tune the circuit.

4.2 Sentence Definition (Standard Form)

This is the three-part classical structure (Term, Class, Characteristics) presented as a single sentence. Best for glossaries, introductions, or when first introducing a core concept.

A firewall is a network security system (Class) that monitors and controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules (Characteristics).

4.3 Extended Definition (Most Detailed Form)

This uses the sentence definition as a starting point, then expands it into a full paragraph or multiple paragraphs using supplementary methods to ensure complete understanding. Best for complex, foundational concepts that require deep understanding.

Supplementary Methods Used in Extended Definitions:
  • Analysis (Principle of Operation): Explaining how it works.
  • Comparison/Contrast: Showing how it is similar or different from related terms.
  • Etymology: The origin of the word.
  • Examples/Illustrations: Providing scenarios or use cases.
  • Negation: Explaining what the term is not.
Extended Definition Example (Paraphrased)

Bandwidth is a measure of the maximum rate of data transfer across a given path (Sentence Definition). It is primarily measured in bits per second (bps) and determines the capacity of a communication link. This term is often confused with speed; however, speed refers to latency, while bandwidth refers to volume (Comparison/Contrast). For example, a water pipe with a large diameter (high bandwidth) can move a large volume of water, even if it moves slowly (Example).

UNIT 5: Technical Writing (Descriptions)

5. Accurate Information Writing Based on Observation

Technical descriptions rely fundamentally on objective, verifiable facts gathered through careful observation, rather than subjective interpretation or opinion.

5.1 Principle of Objectivity

Technical writing must be objective, meaning it focuses solely on observable, measurable reality.

  • Fact, not Opinion: Describe what is, not what you feel or think.
  • Measurable Data: Use specific quantities, dimensions, temperatures, and tolerances. (e.g., "The fan is 10 cm in diameter," not "The fan is large.")
  • Eliminate Bias: Avoid emotionally loaded words or judgmental language.
Objective vs. Subjective

Subjective: "The sensor has a neat little housing and feels rugged."
Objective: "The sensor is encased in injection-molded ABS plastic (ASTM D4549), measuring 3.0 × 1.5 × 0.5 cm, with a Shore D hardness of 80."

6. What Constitutes a Detailed Description?

A description is considered detailed when it provides enough information for the audience to fully visualize, understand the function of, or even replicate the mechanism or process.

6.1 Key Elements of Detail

  • Nomenclature: Use the precise, standard names for all parts.
  • Physical Properties: Detail the material, color, texture, weight, shape, and dimensions.
  • Location and Spatial Relationship: Clearly state where parts are located relative to each other (e.g., "The main gear meshes with the secondary pinion, located 5 mm above the base plate").
  • Function/Purpose: Explain the specific job each part performs within the system.
Checklist for Component Description

Does the description address: What is it? What is it made of? What does it look like (shape/size)? Where is it located? What does it do?

7. Organizing and Presenting a Technical Description

A well-organized description ensures the reader can mentally assemble the parts or follow the steps logically.

7.1 Standard Organizational Structure

a. Introduction

States the definition, overall purpose, and function of the mechanism or process being described. It also lists the main parts.

  • Definition: (Using Unit 4's sentence definition format) What is it?
  • Function: What does it accomplish?
  • Partition: A preview list of the main components or stages that will be described.
b. Body (Detailed Description)

This section describes the components, parts, or stages in a systematic order. The most effective ways to order the body are:

  • Spatial Order: Describing parts from top to bottom, left to right, outside to inside, or front to back. (Best for mechanisms/objects)
  • Functional Order: Describing parts in the sequence they operate. (Best for systems or processes)
  • Chronological Order: Describing steps in the order they occur. (Best for processes)
c. Conclusion

Summarizes the main function and perhaps the significance or application of the mechanism/process.

7.2 Use of Diagrams and Visual Aids

Diagrams are essential in technical descriptions as they provide visual context that text alone cannot match.

[Image of a schematic diagram for a simple circuit]
  • Clarity and Labeling: Every diagram must have clear, accurate labels corresponding to the names used in the text.
  • Placement: Place diagrams as close as possible to the text that references them.
  • Referencing: Always reference the figure in the text (e.g., "As shown in Figure 3.1, the output signal is routed through the operational amplifier...").
  • Types: Use diagrams like exploded views (for assembly), flowcharts (for processes), or schematics (for electrical systems).
